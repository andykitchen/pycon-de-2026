Interventional generalisation

Tools and ideas for answering: if I take action X instead of Y, will I get outcome Z?

Framing question

Central question: If I take action X instead of action Y, will I obtain desired outcome Z?

  • Under uncertainty — limited knowledge, stochastic outcomes
  • Under novelty — settings not seen before, distribution shift

Positioning

  • Toward a methodology for systematic decision making — a direction of travel, not a finished science
  • Make compound, difficult judgments traceable to simpler, less controversial base judgments, repeatably

Why systems? What is systematisation?

  • Systems crystallise judgment: explicit structure for what we believe, observe, and recommend
  • Systematisation — names, relationships, and rules others can inspect, criticise, and extend
  • Systems move judgments around: difficult conclusions from easier premises; disagreement becomes visible

Three conceptual lenses

Lens Typical phrasing Rough formal home
Uncertainty “It’ll be about twenty-something tomorrow” Random variables, distributions, calibration
Causality “If we want X we should do Y” Causal graphs, SCMs, identification
Decision “The best thing to do is X” Decision theory, payoffs, policies, interventional generalisation

Three-step hierarchy

  1. Signal and noise (observational)
  2. Cause and effect (causal / interventional)
  3. Decide and act (utilitarian / prescriptive)

1. Signal and noise

  • Where most of statistics lives: estimation, testing, prediction from data as drawn
  • Highly developed tools (design, inference, UQ for associations and forecasts)

2. Cause and effect

  • Pearl et al.: graphs, do-calculus, SCMs; strong in econometrics / economics
  • Moderately developed; still uncommon outside specialists
  • From “what tends to happen” to “what happens if we intervene”

3. Decide and act

  • Actions under costs, benefits, uncertainty — not only what is true, but what to do
  • Few standard tools; no single widely accepted methodology vs classical stats
  • Links to game theory, especially imperfect information

Two sources of uncertainty

Different responses in models and in process.

Parameters (epistemic / structural)

  • Limits on how well we know the system: form, coefficients, heterogeneity
  • Hierarchical structure, priors, model comparison, scope statements

Noise (aleatoric)

  • Limits predictability of outcomes even with a perfect mechanism model
  • Ceiling on predictability; shapes risk and measurement vs action

Interveneability

  • Fix an outcome variable
  • Compare its distribution: no intervention vs intervention
  • One summary: KL divergence between those distributions (baseline, horizon, conditioning matter)

Generalisation to new situations

Known unknowns

  • Bayesian hierarchical models, random effects, partial pooling
  • Encode what we expect to vary — even when not fully observed

Unknown unknowns

  • Safety margins, explicit conservatism
  • Bias to inaction when downside dominates — require high confidence of net benefit
  • Bias to action in winner-take-all / option-value settings — raise P(best outcome) when passivity loses

Address criticisms

  • False precision — when numbers are theatre; ordinal vs quantitative structure
  • Ethics vs utilitarian framing — whose costs and benefits; non-compensatory values
  • Identification vs ambition — what data can support
  • Governance — ownership, audits, override

Closing

  • Interventional generalisation links causal do(·) to novel regimes — models and values must travel carefully
  • Systems are the scaffolding: inspectable, negotiable
